Core Concept · Layer 3

RAG &
Grounding.

The most practical technique for making AI useful in real businesses. If you only understand one architectural pattern deeply, make it this one.

The problem RAG solves

An LLM trained in 2024 knows a lot — but it doesn't know your product catalog, your return policy, your client list, or anything that happened after its training cutoff. If you ask it about these things without providing them, it will hallucinate a plausible-sounding answer.

RAG — Retrieval-Augmented Generation — solves this by retrieving relevant information from your actual data and including it in the prompt at the moment of the question. The LLM then generates its response based on that real, specific, current information.

The analogy Imagine hiring a brilliant consultant who has read everything ever published — but knows nothing about your company specifically. RAG is like handing them the right pages from your operations manual right before they answer each question. They're still brilliant; now they're also accurate about your business.

How RAG works step by step

1
Index your documents

Your product docs, FAQs, policies, emails, past cases — anything the AI needs to know — are processed into embeddings (numerical representations) and stored in a vector database. This is a one-time setup that updates as your data changes.

2
User asks a question

"What's your return policy for items over $200?" The question is also converted to an embedding.

3
Semantic search retrieves relevant chunks

The vector database finds the document chunks that are semantically closest to the question — even if they use different words. Your return policy document gets retrieved.

4
Chunks are injected into the prompt

"Here is the relevant policy information: [policy text]. Now answer the user's question using only this information." The LLM now has the real answer to work from.

5
LLM generates grounded response

The response is based on your actual policy, not a guess. It can even cite the source. Hallucination risk drops dramatically.

What a vector database is (and why you care)

A vector database stores embeddings — numerical representations of text where similar meaning = similar numbers = stored close together. When you search it, you're doing semantic search: finding content by meaning, not keywords. "Burst pipe emergency" finds your "24-hour plumbing emergency service" content even without exact word matches.

Common vector databases: Pinecone (managed, easy), Chroma (open source, great for starting), Weaviate (open source, more features). For most small business RAG systems, Chroma running locally or Pinecone's free tier is sufficient.

The Monday signal If someone says "the AI keeps giving wrong answers about our products" — the answer is almost always RAG, not fine-tuning, not a different model, not a better prompt. Ground it to your actual product data. That's the fix.
